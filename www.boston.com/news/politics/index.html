Mitt Romney takes aim at “Buffett Rule,” says President Obama’s economic policies have failed American women

His political arrows now aimed squarely at President Obama, Mitt Romney on Wednesday fired away at the proposed “Buffett Rule,” which would force the wealthiest Americans to pay a minimum effective tax rate of 30 percent. “Look, this nation is one nation under God,” he said. “Dividing America is not going to work out.” Romney, trailing Obama two-to-one among female voters, according to polls, also tried to paint the president’s economic policies as harmful to women.

Speaker DeLeo’s budget rejects Governor Patrick’s major initiatives, relies on optimistic assumptions

House Speaker Robert A. DeLeo proposed an annual state spending plan today that rejects several of Governor Deval Patrick’s signature policy proposals, including an overhaul of the community college system and new or increased taxes Patrick had proposed for candy, soda, and cigarettes. The $32.3 billion budget is based on several optimistic assumptions -- including that state tax collections will rebound after they came in lower than expected in five of nine months in the current budget year.

Economic uncertainty will play a key role in presidential tilt, analysts say

Former Mitt Romney adviser Mike Murphy said Tuesday that economic uncertainty makes President Obama vulnerable in his bid for reelection, but he stopped short of predicting a Romney victory. “I’m saying Romney can win,” Murphy said during an appearance at Harvard University’s Belfer Center for Science and International Affairs. Murphy and former Federal Reserve chairman Paul Volcker discussed the role of the economy in the presidential election.

Rick Santorum withholds support for Mitt Romney during exit speech

Rick Santorum uttered many kind words during his exit speech Tuesday afternoon, but not one was directed at Mitt Romney, the man whose path to the Republican presidential nomination Santorum cleared. Santorum consistently refused throughout his run to say he would endorse Romney if he failed to win the GOP nomination, and he made no mention of his rival on Tuesday. Santorum left the stage without answering a reporter’s question about whether he would get behind Romney now.

With Rick Santorum’s exit, Mitt Romney completes eight-year nomination quest

Mitt Romney has spent eight years seeking the Republican presidential nomination, first running for governor of Massachusetts and then a leadership role at the Republican Governors Association before making his first White House bid in 2008. He was the last credible challenger to the eventual nominee, John McCain, and today became that himself as Rick Santorum dropped his own run for the 2012 nomination. It puts Romney in a place where he has sought to be for nearly a decade.
