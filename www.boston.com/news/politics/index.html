Vice President Joe Biden says he’s ‘comfortable’ with gay marriage rights, but can’t promise endorsement of Obama

Vice President Joe Biden on Sunday highlighted President Obama’s advocacy of gay rights and declared his own “comfort” with giving equal rights to same-sex couples, but he stopped short of saying the Obama administration will officially endorse gay marriage in a second term. Obama’s position on same-sex marriage has vacillated over the years. He said last summer that his views are “evolving,” and he has mostly avoided discussing the subject as president.

Former Bain executive’s book on economy sparks interest

Edward Conard’s book “Unintended Consequences: Why Everything You’ve Been Told About the Economy Is Wrong” is not due to appear in bookstores for another month. but the former Bain Capital executive and wealthy Mitt Romney supporter has already sparked some lively conversations. A piece on the book in the New York Times Sunday Magazine, which became public Tuesday, described it as a balm for the 1 percent -- Conard’s take on why wealthy entrepreneurs running successful enterprises is good for the masses.

Tagg Romney announces birth of twin sons through surrogate mother

Mitt Romney’s eldest son, Tagg, made a surprise announcement today: He and his wife, Jen, are the new parents of twin boys delivered by a surrogate. “Happy 2 announce birth of twin boys David Mitt and William Ryder. Big thanks to our surrogate. Life is a miracle,” Tagg Romney said via Twitter at 3:17 p.m. He said on his Facebook page that the surrogate was gestational, meaning the new boys are the biological product of him and his wife.

Maine’s Susan Collins to endorse Scott Brown

Republican US Senator Scott Brown has reeled in an endorsement from the second Maine senator -- Susan Collins. The Maine Republican, who frequently works with Brown as part of an informal caucus of New England Republicans, plans to formally endorse Brown Friday morning at his South Boston campaign headquarters.

Campaign 2012

Democrats rely on record number of women candidates to keep party’s grip on the US Senate

From Massachusetts to Hawaii, Democrats are leaning heavily on their party’s women to retain control of the Senate, which Democrats now control by a razor-thin margin. A record number of Democratic women, including six incumbents, are running for Senate seats - eclipsing by one the 10 that ran in 1992’s “Year of the Woman.’’ The contests come as the political chasm on key social and economic issues widen, and Democrats hope the stark divides will motivate women to go to the polls in droves in November. (Globe Staff, 11:18 p.m.)
